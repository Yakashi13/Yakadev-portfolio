What are “angels” & “demons”?

In religion and folklore, angels and demons are intermediary beings that sit between the divine/sacred realm and everyday human life. In Western traditions, angel (Greek angelos, “messenger”) emphasizes function: carrying messages, guarding, guiding, praising, or executing divine will. Demon comes from Greek daimōn, originally a neutral “spirit” that could influence a person’s character; over time in many traditions the term skewed malevolent. Not all cultures keep the good/evil split—some spirits help or harm depending on context. 0

Cosmic models & spirit types

How the “map of the cosmos” shapes the roster

Beliefs about spirits mirror beliefs about the universe. In some Western views, a tripartite cosmos (heaven/earth/underworld) hosts celestial angels and aerial spirits. Dualistic models pit forces/persons of good against evil. Monistic approaches view all reality as an expression of a single principle, with spirits framed more as functions than factions. 1

What kinds of beings show up?

Besides angels and demons, traditions mention ghosts, goblins, nature spirits, fairies, and hybrid beings. Their status (helpful or hostile) often depends on their alignment with the Supreme Being—or on local ritual relationships. 2

Benevolent • Malevolent • Ambivalent

Benevolent beings

Angels assist: revealing truths, guarding people and nations, escorting souls, and—per popular piety—watching over children. Classic examples include the archangels Gabriel, Michael, Raphael, and Uriel in various canons. 3

Malevolent beings

Demons, fallen angels, and hostile spirits obstruct proper relationship with the divine and can be linked in lore to calamity, temptation, and disorder. Visual culture often depicts them as hybrid or monstrous to warn or ward off. 4

Ambivalent / neutral beings

Islam explicitly includes jinn—beings of smokeless fire—who can help or harm; many nature spirits in nonliterate traditions are treated as neutral and “made friendly” through ritual. 5

From Zoroastrianism to Abrahamic faiths

Early Zoroastrianism names a heptad of holy “bounteous immortals” (amesha spentas) aligned with Ahura Mazdā; against them stands Angra Mainyu (Ahriman) and the daevas. Jewish and later Christian and Islamic thought interacted with Persian ideas during/after the Babylonian Exile, enriching hierarchies of angels (e.g., nine choirs in later Christian mysticism) and sharpening demonology (Satan gaining the role of arch-adversary). 6

Named figures include Michael and Gabriel (canon), with Raphael and Uriel in certain apocrypha; folk practice adds charms and amulets to counter unclean spirits. 7

Lucifer, Satan & the seven

In many Christian retellings, pride or rebellion explains the “fall” of angels (Lucifer → Satan). Medieval demonology often lists seven archdemons mapped to the seven deadly sins: Lucifer (Pride), Mammon (Avarice), Asmodeus (Lust), Satan (Wrath), Beelzebub (Gluttony), Leviathan (Envy), Belphegor (Sloth). Demons tempt, deceive, and—per older views—afflict with calamities or possession. 8

Related topics: Beelzebub as a demonized Philistine deity; the Nephilim tradition linking “sons of God” and human women in some interpretations (esp. 1 Enoch). 9

Hindu, Buddhist, Chinese & Japanese views

In South and East Asia, “angel-like” work is often handled by avatars and bodhisattvas—agents that extend sacred action into the world. Hindu myths set asuras against devas, with episodes like the amrita contest (Vishnu as Mohini). Demons that harass humans include rakshasas and pishachas. In Buddhism, Māra tempts the Buddha; later traditions absorb local spirit catalogues. Chinese religion speaks of guei-shen across nature; Japanese lore features powerful oni and possessive tengu. 10

The Great Rebellion

At the center of angelic lore stands the rebellion: a cosmic fracture in the heavenly order. The most famous tale is of Lucifer, the “light-bearer,” whose pride and refusal to submit marked the birth of the fallen. Jewish texts also preserve the legend of the Watchers, angels who descended to Earth, took human wives, and spawned giants. Their corruption set the stage for divine judgment, echoing across later Christian and Islamic demonologies.

Demons of the Middle Ages

In the medieval imagination, demons were not abstract — they stalked daily life. Sleep paralysis was blamed on incubi and succubi, spirits said to feed on desire and fear. Monastic texts detail fierce exorcisms, while grimoires like the Key of Solomon circulated lists of infernal names and sigils. The age blurred theology with folklore, creating a Europe where angels guarded monasteries and demons whispered through the dark forests.

Archangel Michael & the War

Michael, “who is like God,” emerges in Jewish, Christian, and Islamic texts as the cosmic general. In the Book of Revelation, he leads heaven’s armies against the dragon — a symbol of Satan and his host. Artistic depictions show him armored, wings blazing, sword in hand. To many traditions, he embodies divine justice, not just a protector of souls but the very figure who ensures evil is cast down forever.

The Watchers' Descent

In ancient texts, the Watchers were angels who descended to Earth, attracted by the beauty of human women. Their union led to the birth of the Nephilim, giants who were said to have corrupted humanity. This act of defiance against divine order resulted in their punishment and imprisonment.

Necromancy in the Middle Ages

Necromancy, the practice of communicating with the dead, was prevalent in medieval Europe. Grimoires like the Picatrix and the Sworn Book of Honorius detailed rituals to summon spirits and seek knowledge from the afterlife. These practices were often condemned by the Church but remained popular among certain circles.

The Fall of the Rebel Angels

The rebellion in heaven, led by Lucifer, resulted in a celestial war. Michael and his angels fought against the dragon and his followers. The battle culminated in the expulsion of the rebels from heaven, a pivotal moment that shaped the cosmic order and the eternal struggle between good and evil.

Exorcism Rituals in Medieval Europe

Exorcism rituals in medieval Europe were elaborate ceremonies conducted to expel demons from the possessed. These rituals often involved prayers, holy water, and the use of relics. The Church played a central role in these practices, and successful exorcisms were seen as signs of divine intervention.

The Demonization of the Other

Throughout history, societies have often portrayed outsiders—be they different in culture, religion, or appearance—as demonic. This demonization served to justify exclusion, persecution, and violence. Understanding this pattern helps in recognizing and combating prejudice in modern times.

Mazzikin — Invisible Mischief Makers

In Jewish Talmudic lore, mazzikin are invisible demons whose name means “damagers” or “those who harm.” They can create nuisances or outright dangers in daily life—everything from tripping you to whispering terrifying messages. One Talmudic sage claimed every person is flanked by thousands of them. Ancient protective magic like incantation bowls were designed to ward them off. 0

Harut & Marut — Fallen Teachers

In Islamic tradition, Harut and Marut were angels sent to test humanity by teaching magic on Earth—but they too were swayed by temptation. Earthly desires broke their angelic grace. They chose earthly punishment rather than spiritual exile, offering a moral tale about temptation and responsibility. 1

Succubus & Incubus — Dream Demons

In folklore, an incubus is a male demon that visits sleeping women, while a succubus is the female counterpart, seducing men in dreams. These entities drain vitality—sometimes to the point of death—and in myth, their unions with humans could produce a hybrid known as a cambion. 2

Nephilim — Giants of Old

The Nephilim are mythical figures described as the offspring of “sons of God” (interpreted as angels) and human women. Known as giants or “fallen ones,” they feature in biblical accounts and later legends, sparking endless fascination in literature, gaming, and pop culture. 3

Angelic Order Fact

Biblical texts imply that angels are ranked—from fiery seraphim to majestic cherubim and authoritative archangels. Each rank carries a different role: worship, protection, guidance. This structure permeates theological writings and art. 4
